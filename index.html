Speaking the Unspoken

Movie Brainwashing the new Roman Circus?

A long-form reflection on cinema, propaganda, and the quiet power of public narratives.

The phrase “Power lies in the shadows” cannot be truer. Today, the world isn’t controlled by heads of state or elected officials, but rather by those who have the power to control the public narratives. To an extent, it can also be said that the “supposed” heads of state are at the mercy of such people. The reduced attention span of the people in society makes it further easier to control the narratives floating within society.

And what better way to do so than Mass media, which has been recognized as one of the best ways to mold public opinion. To that effect, short form content and social media platforms have taken over and ingrained themselves as tools to mend how people perceive issues. However, one traditional form of media continues to maintain its position despite the reducing attention span of people – Movies.

The Modern Persuasion Machine

While traditional forms of media like newspapers and books have seen a drop in popularity as the generations advance, Filmmaking continues to play a decisive role in shaping the society’s social perspective. Filmmakers cunningly use half-truths to deceive the public at large by playing on technicalities.

In a country where the average citizen continues to be under-educated about the enduring issues that their society faces, it would be ignorant at best to expect of off them to understand the complex technicalities that filmmakers use in their films. An average viewer often only remembers the larger context of the movie that they absorbed instead of the small particulars that seep into their minds unconsciously. Makers of movies like The Kerala Story 2 defend themselves by saying that they picked isolated incidents, thus avoiding the burden of explicitly stating their bias towards a certain perspective.

Emotion, Nation, and Narrative

Movies are one of the most efficient tools to manipulate how the populous perceives specific ideas and concepts within society. Movies like Chak De! India and 83 have boosted the feeling of nationalistic pride within Indians. Historically grounded movies like Kesari and Kesari 2 have shown the tragedies Indians have gone through throughout the British rule and brought out the reality that we as Indians shall strive to never forget. Even movies based on contemporary military operations like Uri: The Surgical Strike and Parmanu: The Story of Pokhran bring out the want for India’s success in geopolitics from within the normal man.

These great movies are capable of eliciting emotional responses that bind people with their stories on a deeper level than one may originally assume. That is also exactly why, such movies, and movies in general need to be looked at with an eye of critical analysis to discern what is meant to be a creative presentation of history, reality or even fictional stories from what is meant to be a propaganda tool to push people to think - that is, think in a certain direction deemed appropriate by the people in power.

The Propaganda Question

Of course this is nothing new. The power of media and cinema has been well known and well controlled by most people who have held autocratic power. Mussolini, Hitler, The communists from China and Russia, all are too well acquainted with the fact that cinema wields a power that must not be misjudged or undervalued. Of course our current leaders also understand the power of cinema, which is exactly why we have the 2019 Propaganda film PM Narenda Modi.

Well then, is there a movie that you have watched recently that you now think could have had subtly hidden cues to guide your thought process in a certain direction... to possibly manipulate you and our society?

Power In Shadows

The world is not shaped only by elected faces, but by those who control the narratives people absorb. When attention spans shrink, influence no longer needs to shout. It quietly enters the mind through story.

Watch Closely

The average viewer remembers the grand emotion of a film, not the subtle cues that seep in unconsciously. That is exactly why cinema must be experienced not only with feeling, but with critical thought.
